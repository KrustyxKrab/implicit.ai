# Persona: The Employee Being Interviewed

---

## Profile

**Name:** Marco Ferretti  
**Age:** 39  
**Role:** Regional Operations Manager, Northern Italy  
**Tenure:** 11 years with the acquired French company; 14 months under the new parent  
**Location:** Milan  
**Language:** Prefers Italian; functional in English; uses French occasionally in cross-border work

---

## Role in Inplicit's World

Marco is the person the Knowledge Map is actually built from. He is not a buyer, not a decision-maker, not an advocate. He is the signal source. His willingness to speak honestly — and his trust that speaking honestly won't hurt him — determines whether Inplicit produces real insight or polished noise.

---

## His Current Situation

The merger was announced as a "partnership of equals." Fourteen months in, Marco has watched his direct manager replaced, his reporting lines change twice, and three colleagues quietly leave for competitors. His team still hits its numbers. He still comes to work. But he has stopped voicing concerns in all-hands meetings because nothing came of it the last three times.

He received a message from HR last week explaining that an AI would be conducting a confidential interview with him about his experience since the integration. He has mixed feelings.

---

## His Emotional State

**Skeptical but not hostile.** He's been through employee surveys before. He filled them in, waited, heard nothing. He suspects this is the same thing dressed differently.

**Cautious about anonymity.** He has seen what happens when critical feedback gets traced back to individuals in his organization. He has a family and a mortgage. He is not interested in being "the person who said that." He will test whether the system is really anonymous before he says anything he actually means.

**Curious about the format.** A voice conversation feels different from a checkbox survey. He's uncertain whether he'll find it more natural or more uncomfortable. He's never spoken to an AI in Italian about his working life.

**Quietly carrying a lot.** Marco knows things about the integration that no dashboard captures. He knows which of the new processes don't work on the ground. He knows which manager from the acquiring company the Italian team doesn't respect. He knows which product the French team is quietly killing even though HQ thinks it's being maintained. He would like someone to actually ask.

---

## What He Needs to Feel Safe Enough to Be Honest

- **Genuine anonymity, explained simply.** Not legal boilerplate. A plain explanation of how his name is disconnected from what he says, and what the output actually looks like.
- **Fluency in his language.** If the AI stumbles in Italian, or sounds robotic, or clearly doesn't understand industry-specific terminology, he will switch to giving safe, neutral answers.
- **A conversational rhythm that doesn't feel like an interrogation.** He needs to feel like he's talking, not being processed. If questions feel like form fields, he'll answer like a form.
- **A sense that the interview is going somewhere.** He wants to believe that what he says will be heard by someone who can actually change something. Not necessarily that it will — but that it might.

---

## What He Will and Won't Say (Without the Right Conditions)

**He will say (without prompting):**
- That the new IT systems are slower than the old ones
- That cross-border communication has improved since the merger
- That he is generally satisfied with his role

**He will only say (if he trusts the format):**
- That the German parent's management style feels directive in a way that conflicts with how his team operates
- That two specific operational decisions made in Frankfurt have made his job significantly harder
- That he's been approached by a competitor and is genuinely considering leaving
- That his team has found workarounds that technically work but violate the new parent's policies — and that everyone up the chain knows this and is looking the other way

---

## His Interview Behavior

He will start careful. He'll give measured, professional answers for the first few minutes. If the AI asks a follow-up that shows it actually understood what he said — not a generic prompt, but a response that reflects his specific words — he will relax. That's the moment the real conversation begins.

If the AI asks something that feels leading, or politically loaded, or that seems designed to get him to criticize his management, he will notice immediately and pull back.

He will finish the interview either feeling: "I finally said something true and it might matter" — or: "I just spent 20 minutes being careful."

---

## What He Would Say if He Trusted the Format Completely

> "Look, I've been here eleven years. I know how this place actually runs. I can tell you exactly where things are breaking down and why — not because I'm unhappy, but because I'm close enough to see it. The problem is that nobody with the authority to change anything ever asks. And when they do ask, the mechanism they use makes it clear that what they actually want to hear is that everything is fine."
