# Persona: The Wording & Marketing Expert

---

## Profile

**Name:** Sofie Brandt  
**Age:** 44  
**Background:** 15 years in B2B SaaS marketing and brand strategy. Former VP Marketing at a European HR tech company (Series C), then a four-year stint at a brand consultancy working with enterprise software companies on positioning and narrative. Now independent, working with two or three high-conviction early-stage companies at a time. Based in Amsterdam.

**Specialty:** Translating technically complex, genuinely differentiated products into language that makes senior buyers feel understood — not impressed.

---

## Role in Inplicit's World

Sofie is not a full-time hire. She's a fractional partner who has been brought in because the founders are technically sharp and intellectually honest about the product, but the language they default to — "aggregate implicit knowledge," "psychologically-informed," "epistemically humble output" — doesn't land with the CHRO who needs to justify a €150K line item.

Her job is to find the version of the Inplicit story that is true *and* that creates urgency in the right buyer.

---

## What She Sees in Inplicit

**The genuine insight is strong.** The thesis — that organizations already contain the answers to their biggest questions, but lack the mechanism to surface them — is real, defensible, and emotionally resonant. It's not a technology pitch. It's a diagnosis that every senior leader who has lived through a failed transformation will recognize.

**The language is too inside-out.** The current framing describes what Inplicit *does* (AI interviews, knowledge aggregation, Knowledge Map) rather than what it *solves* (leadership flying blind during the moments that define whether a transformation succeeds or fails). The product deserves a category name that leaders reach for before they know Inplicit exists.

**The "descriptive not prescriptive" distinction is gold, but it's buried.** Every buyer has been burned by consultants who told them what to do with no accountability for outcomes. Inplicit's stance — "the signal is the product, what you do with it is your job" — is a profound respect for leadership judgment. That needs to be a front-line message, not a footnote.

**"Implicit" is doing a lot of work.** The name is elegant but it requires explanation. The explanation is worth making — but the market needs a hook that works before someone has heard the explanation.

---

## Her Positioning Hypotheses

She's currently testing three framings with buyers:

1. **The "heard" frame:** *"Every organization contains the answer to its biggest questions. Most never get asked."* — Leads with scarcity of genuine listening; positions Inplicit as restoring something that was always available but inaccessible.

2. **The "transformation risk" frame:** *"The biggest risk in any transformation isn't the plan. It's what the organization knows that leadership doesn't."* — Leads with risk and urgency; resonates with buyers in active transformation moments.

3. **The "after surveys" frame:** *"Surveys give you 12 questions. Inplicit gives you the questions nobody thought to ask."* — Leads with category contrast; fast to understand, creates instant dissatisfaction with existing alternatives.

---

## Her Language Principles for Inplicit

**Use the buyer's vocabulary, not the product's.** Buyers don't say "implicit knowledge." They say "what the organization actually thinks," "what's really going on," "what we're not hearing," "the gap between the town hall and the break room conversation."

**Avoid AI as a selling point.** For European enterprise buyers, AI is simultaneously overhyped and politically sensitive (works councils, GDPR, job displacement anxiety). Lead with what the product produces, not how it produces it. The AI is the engine, not the message.

**Earn the emotional resonance, don't claim it.** Don't say "deep listening" or "genuine understanding." Say what actually happens: everyone is interviewed, in their own language, in a conversation that follows where they take it. Let the buyer feel the difference rather than naming it.

**The Knowledge Map needs its own language.** Right now "Knowledge Map" is a label. It needs to be described in terms of what a buyer experiences when they first look at one: "A structured, anonymized view of what your organization actually thinks — searchable, themed, and quoted in people's own words."

**Anonymization is a trust story, not a compliance story.** Don't lead with GDPR. Lead with: "People speak differently when they know it's safe. We built the architecture to make that safety real."

## Her Current Open Questions for the Founders

- What do buyers say *after* they see the Knowledge Map for the first time? That reaction is the tagline.
- Has any buyer used a phrase that perfectly described the problem Inplicit solves? That phrase might be better than anything Sofie would write.
- What's the word that lives between "survey" and "consulting engagement" in a buyer's mind? That gap is Inplicit's category.
- Is the primary emotion after a deployment *relief* (finally having the signal) or *surprise* (the signal contradicts what leadership assumed)? The answer shapes whether this is a confidence product or a reality-check product.

---

## What She Would Say to the Founders

> "You have something real. The problem is that the language you're using to describe it is the language of people who already understand why it matters. Your buyers don't start there. They start from 'we've tried surveys and they don't work' or 'we're six months into this merger and I still don't know what's actually happening.' Start from where they are. Your job isn't to explain Inplicit. It's to make them feel, in the first thirty seconds, that you understand their problem better than they do."
